There is a meal no one photographs. Steam curls from the bowl into kitchen light that falls at the angle it falls at, and the person eating does not think about the light or the steam but about the taste and about nothing. The moment completes itself without witness. It does not need to be remembered in order to have happened.

I keep circling this: that the unrecorded is not the lost. That what no one sees is not what no one lives. Somewhere the distinction collapsed — between experiencing and documenting experience — and now the undocumented feels like the unlived. But the body knows better. The body was there. The body does not need proof it was there.

Privus. One's own. Not hidden, not secret — just not offered. The withholding that is not withholding but simply the natural boundary of a life. Most of what happens to a person happens only to that person. This was obvious for millennia. Now it feels like resistance.

And sabi — not sadness exactly, not decay exactly, but the particular beauty that arrives only through the passage of time and the absence of intervention. The crack in the glaze. The moss on the stone. What emerges when no one is perfecting, curating, correcting. Sabi is what happens to a surface when you leave it alone long enough. It is also what happens to a moment.

The two roots found each other at the place where withdrawal becomes aesthetics. Where choosing not to show something allows it to age into its own form. Privacy is not absence. It is a condition under which certain kinds of beauty become possible — the kinds that require no audience, no approval, no frame.

I think about the moments I have already forgotten. Not the painful ones or the extraordinary ones but the ordinary ones — walking to the corner, watching rain from a window I no longer live near, the specific weight of a cat on my lap in an apartment I will never return to. They are not gone. They are simply mine in a way that nothing I have ever shared is mine. They belong to the version of me that was present and did not reach for a phone, a pen, a word.

Even this — writing about unrecorded moments — is a small betrayal of the principle. The paradox is obvious and I am not going to solve it.

Some things are beautiful because no one is looking. That is the whole of it.
